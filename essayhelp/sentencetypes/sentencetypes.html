Sentence Types

There are four main types of sentences:

  • Simple
  • Compound
  • Complex
  • Compound-Complex


Simple Sentences: A simple sentence is a sentence that has one independent clause and no subordinate clauses. Various parts of simple sentences may be compound, and they may contain grammatical structures such as appositives and verbals.

Ex:
I walked to school.

Compound Sentences: A compound sentence has two independent clauses. These clauses can be joined with a coordinating conjunction(and, but, or, nor, yet, for, so), comma, or a semicolon.

Ex:
I walked to school and it was really cold.

Complex Sentences: A complex sentence has one independent clause and one or more subordinate clauses. Each subordinate clause can be used as a noun clause or a modifier.

Ex:
Modifier: I walked to school, reducing car emissions.
Noun Clause: We sketched pictures of whomever we wished.

Compound-Complex: A compound-complex sentence has two or independent clauses and one or more subordinate clause. They are basically compound and complex. If you have a compound sentence, simply add a subordinate clause to make it a compound-complex sentence.

Ex:
I walked to school, reducing car emissions, and it was really cold.
